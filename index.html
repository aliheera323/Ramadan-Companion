Intentions of the Fast

Today's Niyyat

Two intentions bookend your day of Roza — one to begin, one to complete.

Before Suhoor

Niyyat al-Roza — Intention to Fast

نَوَيْتُ صَوْمَ غَدٍ مِنْ شَهْرِ رَمَضَانَ الْمُبَارَكِ فَرْضًا لِلَّهِ تَعَالَى

"Nawaytu sawma ghadin min shahri Ramadan al-Mubaraki fardhan lillahi ta'ala."

In plain English: "I intend to fast tomorrow as the obligatory fast of the blessed month of Ramadan, for the sake of Allah, the Most High."

Niyyat means "intention." In Islamic practice, every act of worship — especially fasting — must be accompanied by a conscious intention in your heart. The fast isn't just skipping food; it's a deliberate act of devotion.


In the Dawoodi Bohra tradition, the Niyyat for Roza is ideally said the night before or at Suhoor. You can say it quietly in your heart or aloud — what matters is that you've consciously committed to the fast for the sake of Allah.


Think of it like this: eating a meal isn't the same as eating Suhoor. The Niyyat is what transforms a meal into an act of worship.

At Iftar · 7:14 PM

Niyyat al-Iftaar — Intention to Break Fast

اللَّهُمَّ لَكَ صُمْتُ وَعَلَى رِزْقِكَ أَفْطَرْتُ

"Allahumma laka sumtu wa 'ala rizqika aftartu."

In plain English: "O Allah, for You I fasted, and with Your provision I break my fast."

This Niyyat is said at the moment of Iftar — ideally just before the first sip of water or bite of food, precisely when Maghrib begins (7:14 PM today).


In the Dawoodi Bohra tradition, it is customary to break the fast with water and a date, recite this Niyyat, and then offer Maghrib Namaz before the main Iftar meal. The Niyyat acknowledges that the fast was for Allah's sake, and that even the food you now eat is a provision from Him.


It completes the intention set at Suhoor — together, the two Niyyat frame the entire day of Roza as a conscious, bookended act of worship.
